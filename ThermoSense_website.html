The problem

Two-wheeler EVs fail thermally — and current diagnostics see it too late

Most thermal incidents are preceded by gradual, detectable degradation, not sudden failure. The diagnostics in use today aren't built to see that stage.

Signal methods see symptoms, not causes

Motor Current Signature Analysis flags faults from current harmonics — but typically only once they're already pronounced.

Model-based methods assume fixed dynamics

Most diagnostic models assume a motor behaves the same way across its lifetime. In practice, it doesn't.

Aging, load, and heat shift the dynamics

Aging, changing load, and thermal stress continuously change the underlying system before a full failure occurs — and nothing tracks that drift.

The gap

Nobody is tracking how the motor's own dynamics drift over time

The opportunity: track interpretable model coefficients as they evolve, and catch the drift stage — well before failure.
Our solution

An adaptive, physics-interpretable early-warning framework

Adaptive system identification

Sliding-window identification continuously re-estimates the motor's governing coefficients from live signals, instead of assuming they're fixed.

Signal-based cross-validation

Motor Current Signature Analysis runs alongside, validating physical-model findings against current-harmonic evidence.

Early, interpretable alerts

Each tracked coefficient maps to a real physical quantity — an alert tells you which mechanism is degrading, not just that something is.

Validation to date

The core method is proven; the EV motor application is the next step

Proven on a benchmark hybrid hydraulic fault-isolation testbed

  • Multiple fault types — leak, actuator degradation, valve fouling — isolated from coefficient drift alone
  • Coefficients recovered to within ~2% of true values under realistic noise
  • Switchable-component faults isolate with high confidence — genuine signature separation, not just detection

What the Propel program funds
